Critical Analysis & Refinement of the Small Punch Test

As the capstone of my engineering degree, this project explores the advancement of the Small Punch Test (SPT) as a tool for understanding and tracking material degradation. By integrating experimental data, computational modeling, and numerical analysis, I am developing a more accurate and efficient method for assessing how materials degrade over time. This work not only enhances predictive maintenance and failure analysis in engineering systems but also highlights my expertise in Finite Element Analysis (FEA), material science, and advanced simulation techniques. Dive in to explore the progress so far and the potential impact of this research on engineering diagnostics.

Project Overview

Currently in progress, this project aims to study and enhance a materials testing method known as The Small Punch Test (SPT). Broadly, this research pertains to the development and transition between internal stress states that emerge throughout a small punch test. The specific focus if to further the understanding of specimen response behavior at different loading stages for a range of materials with varying ductility. The small punch tests inherent sensitivit to ductility entails prospective use to accurately track operational damage to engineering systems. To better understand relevant SPT behavior mechanisms a Finite Element Model is developed and analysed. This is used hand in hand with experimental data and theoretical SPT analysis methods to critically analyze the contemporary definitions of a specimen's behavior throughout a small punch test. The desired outcome, on a small scale, is to develop a more reliable toolset (FEA model included) to conduct small punch testing. On a larger scale, the reduced material demand and the potential for improved understanding of the test method’s capabilities could help reduce waste and costs in the maintenance of engineering systems.

Challenges & Solutions

The project faced technical difficulties when the model was initially being developed in ANSYS Parametric Design Language (APDL). Progress was hindered by a lack of colleagues with sufficient expertise in APDL and a shortage of online resources to troubleshoot coding issues. Without adequate support, resolving errors and refining the model became increasingly challenging. To overcome this, the focus was shifted to ANSYS Mechanical, which provided a more intuitive development environment with better documentation. This transition allowed for more efficient model development, ensuring the project remained on track to meet deadlines.

The initial prototype model presented several shortcomings. The main issues were discrepancies between experimental and simulated force-displacement curves, as well as invalid stress concentrations stemming from convergence issues and simple bilinear material models. To overcome these limitations, boundary conditions were refined, and Normal Lagrange methods were implemented for better contact accuracy. Further, multilinear isotropic hardening models were created with multilinear material data sourced from peer-reviewed journals.

Outcomes & Results

The project is still a work in progress. So far a Finite Element Model has been developed following specimen and apparatus dimensions from ASTM E3205- 20. The overall system has been simplified to an axisymmetric section to reduce computational demand as shown below.

The model uses a force driven die defined with transient loading conditions to simulate quasi-static loading on the test specimen. Various materials are simulated using multilinear isotropic hardening models with data sourced from peer reviewed journals. Experimental Small Punch Test data has been collected from at QUT. As of now the model can output SPT results qualitatively similar to experimental data of materials with moderate ductility. A summary chart of both simulated and experimental results is presented below.

The image above is an overlay of multiple simulated SPT instances from Ansys Mechanical, as well as experimental data from 316L Steel. As shown the model is sensitive to the variation in material properties and holds true to the expectation of punch resistance increasing with material strength.

The stress states that emerge in the specimen through simulation also exhibit expected behavior as it pertains to critical failure locations on a sample. The location of failure for 316L samples can be seen just just below and slightly out from the center.

The simulated counterpart for 316L shows critical stress propagating in a manner analogous to that of experimental samples.

The next step is to broaden the functionality of the model to highly brittle and highly ductile materials. Subsequently, material property and SPT data will be collected experimentally with a set of sample materials spanning a range of ductility. Material property data will be used to redefine materials models and results from subsequent simulations will be compared to those collected experimentally. Should all go as planned this will serve as a checkpoint from which a final model can be derived, and study of stress state propagation can begin.
